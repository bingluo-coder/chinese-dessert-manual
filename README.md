# Chinese Dessert & Snack Manual

Welcome to a small, tasty tour through Chinese desserts, pastries, teas, and crunchy snacks.

This manual is not trying to be a textbook. It is more like opening a snack drawer with a friend: a little sweet, a little fragrant, sometimes spicy, and always worth exploring.

## Start Here If You Want...

**Something traditional:** try the [Mooncakes](mooncake/README.md), especially the rose red bean or five-nut flavors.

**Something fruity:** visit [Fengli Su & Assorted Pastries](fenglisu/README.md) for pineapple cake, mango milk cake, and coconut pastry notes.

**Something to drink:** open [Chagee Tea Gift Sets](chagee/README.md) for black tea, green tea, oolong, fruit tea, and cold-brew ideas.

**Something crunchy and local:** check [Chongqing Snacks](chongqingsnaks/README.md) for typical snacks we eat often in Chongqing: guaiwei broad beans, mahua, and spicy potato chips.

## Tiny Flavor Guide

If you like floral flavors, look for rose, jasmine, and tea blossom.

If you like creamy flavors, start with coconut pastry, mango milk cake, or milk tea ideas.

If you like fruit flavors, pineapple, peach, honeydew, grape, and mango show up across the collection.

If you like spice, the Chongqing snack pile has chili, Sichuan pepper, and plenty of crunch.

## Best Way To Use This Manual

Pick a folder, look at the pictures, read the short notes, and decide what sounds fun to taste first. Snacks are better when curiosity leads the way.
